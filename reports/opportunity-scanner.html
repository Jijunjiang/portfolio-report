The finding that matters

Drawdown depth alone does not discriminate. PTON's −98.4% drawdown is statistically indistinguishable from CVNA's −99.2% and worse than HOOD's −92% — a pure price/technical score would have rated it at least as attractive as the three winners. It wasn't. What differed was qualitative: HOOD, INTC, and CVNA all had cyclical or operationally fixable problems (trading-volume cyclicality, a fixable manufacturing execution issue, an over-levered balance sheet fixed by restructuring). PTON's collapse reflects structural demand destruction — a pandemic-era spike unwinding permanently, not a cyclical trough waiting to mean-revert.

Honest limit: fundamentals data is a current-only snapshot, so this distinction was validated narratively, not backtested mechanically — which is exactly why the rubric makes it a mandatory judgment check, not a footnote.

The rubric — 31 scored criteria + 2 gates, 5 categories

M mechanical (computed directly from a tool) · J judgment (requires reading/context). Roughly two-thirds mechanical, one-third judgment — score guide: ≥70 strong candidate, 50–69 watchlist, <50 discard.

Two criteria are gates, not scores (added 2026-07-07)

Net profit margin > 0 is enforced identically for every candidate before it ever reaches the rubric (it's a Stage-0 scan filter on all four archetypes), so scoring it again downstream could never discriminate anything — retired as a point criterion. Domain leadership (a top-tier or solid niche market position, durable enough to hold through a full 10-year cycle) disqualifies a candidate outright if it's sub-scale/commoditized/easily-disrupted, rather than just costing it points — a real requirement, not a nice-to-have averaged into the total.

Daily signal — gate first, then mechanical monitoring, quarterly deep review

Every archetype's Stage-0 survivors get the domain-leadership gate applied first (turnaround also gets the 40%-off-high gate) — disqualified candidates are logged and dropped before any scoring effort is spent on them. Full scoring (categories B/D judgment calls included) stays quarterly for anything past the gates — that research doesn't belong in a daily job, and this account only acts a few times a quarter regardless. The no-judgment-call criteria (Category A for turnaround, the equivalent mechanical criteria for the other three archetypes) run daily as part of the portfolio report: a cheap watch for something new crossing into interesting territory, escalated to a full review the same day it happens rather than sitting unnoticed for months.

Refining the rubric over time

Not an automated optimizer — there isn't enough resolved-outcome volume for that to be honest. Instead: every scored or gate-disqualified candidate is logged to reports/opportunity-scanner-logs/ (one CSV per run, score/gate outcome, category subtotals, price at scan). Each quarter, prior candidates get their actual return checked against the accumulated logs, and weights or thresholds get adjusted when the evidence supports it — never off fewer than ~5 resolved cases per category, to avoid fitting noise. Call it hill-climbing in spirit: a human-and-agent review loop paced to match how often this account actually acts.
